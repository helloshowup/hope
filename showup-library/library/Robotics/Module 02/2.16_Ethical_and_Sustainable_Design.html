2.16

Ethical and Sustainable Design

Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, you'll be able to:

  • Analyze the environmental impacts of materials commonly used in robotics throughout their lifecycle
  • Explain at least three approaches that improve end-of-life management for robotic systems
  • Compare different materials used in robotics based on performance, sustainability, and ethical considerations

 

The materials we use to build robots affect more than just how well they work. These choices have important effects on our environment and society. As robots become more common in our world, we need to design them with sustainability in mind. Ethical material selection means thinking about the entire life of a robot—from getting the raw materials, to making it, using it, and finally recycling or throwing it away.

 

Robots present special challenges for sustainability because they contain many different materials all put together. Unlike simple products, robots typically mix metals, plastics, electronics, and special materials in ways that can be hard to take apart later. This means we need to think carefully about how to design robots so they can be taken apart and their materials reused when they're no longer needed.

 

Many robot companies are now using ideas from the "circular economy," which means designing products so their materials can be reused instead of wasted. This approach is not just good for the planet—it can also save money on materials, help companies follow environmental rules, and create a positive image with customers who care about the environment.

Environmental Impact of Materials

The environmental effects of robotics start with getting the raw materials, which can harm nature in several ways. Mining for metals like aluminum, copper, and rare earth elements (which are common in robots) can destroy natural habitats, pollute water, and use lots of energy. Similarly, plastics come from oil, and getting oil from the ground can harm the environment.

 

Think about your smartphone or tablet. These devices contain tiny amounts of rare metals that require big mining operations to get. Robots often use these same materials, and engineers are becoming more aware of how their choices affect our planet.

 

Making robot parts adds more environmental concerns. Processing materials often uses lots of energy, requires chemical treatments, and creates waste. For example, carbon fiber is super strong and lightweight, but making it uses lots of energy and it's hard to recycle. Electronic parts in robots contain valuable metals and sometimes harmful substances that need careful handling throughout their life.

 

The global supply chain makes these impacts even bigger, with materials often mined in one country, processed in another, and assembled in yet another—each step adding pollution from transportation. Responsible material selection tries to reduce these impacts by using locally sourced materials, recycled content, or alternatives that cause less harm when they're extracted. Some robot companies now use special tools to measure environmental impacts when choosing materials, looking at things like carbon footprint and water usage alongside performance.

Recyclability and Waste

End-of-life issues are especially challenging for robots because of how they're built. Unlike simpler products, robots typically combine multiple materials that are connected with glues, overmolding, or composite structures that can be difficult to separate for recycling. Electronic components contain valuable materials like gold, silver, and copper, but getting these out efficiently requires special processes.

 

"Design for disassembly" is a new approach that thinks about how products can be taken apart at the end of their life to recover materials. This idea affects material choices by favoring connections that can be undone (like screws) instead of permanent bonds (like glue), and using material combinations that don't interfere with recycling. For example, using screws instead of glue to attach parts, or making sure different types of plastics are clearly labeled and easy to separate.

 

"Design for circularity" takes this idea even further by thinking about how materials might be recovered and reused in future products. This might mean selecting materials that can be recycled many times without losing their properties, or designing parts that can be fixed and reused directly. Some robot companies are exploring modular designs where individual parts can be upgraded or replaced without throwing away the entire robot, making it last longer and creating less waste.

 

Biodegradable materials are another way to address end-of-life concerns, especially for robots used in nature or farming where getting them back might be hard. These materials are designed to break down safely in specific environments, though they usually don't perform as well as regular materials and must be carefully matched to how long the robot needs to last and where it will be used.

Material Selection Strategies

Robot engineers use several strategies to balance performance, cost, and sustainability when choosing materials. One common approach is to create a decision chart that weighs different factors based on how important they are for that specific robot. For example, a robot designed for a school might prioritize durability and safety over maximum performance, while a competition robot might prioritize performance above all else.

 

Another strategy is to use different materials for different parts of the robot based on what each part needs to do. For instance, a robot might use:

  • Aluminum for the main frame (good strength for its weight)
  • ABS plastic for protective covers (resists impacts and is easy to shape)
  • Rubber for wheels (good grip and absorbs shocks)
  • Special materials only where absolutely necessary

 

Engineers also think about how parts will be made when selecting materials. Some materials might have great properties but require special equipment or skills to work with. For school and hobby robotics, materials that can be worked with simple tools are often preferred, even if they don't offer the absolute best performance.

 

By carefully thinking about these factors and using a step-by-step approach to material selection, robot engineers can create designs that balance performance needs with practical limits and ethical considerations.
